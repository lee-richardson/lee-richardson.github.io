Creating a scalable design system

What was the project?

To improve workflow inefficiencies, I built a design system so designers can work faster.

My role

  • — Visual and accessibility audit.
  • — Design system organisation.
  • — Component creation.

Description

Over a couple of sprints I worked with another designer to audit a brand and create a design system.

The problem

Designers had to learn and monitor all of the subtle and evolving differences in design, UX and behaviours specific to the brand.

The outcome

A Figma design system and components that drastically sped up internal processes.

Research

The design team relied on meetings, syncs and group decisions to maintain a cohesive UI and consistent UX patterns. This was working but barely, with designers often waiting until a meeting or group-call to confirm on components and design patterns. This was proving to be inefficient.


To streamline the team's workflow and improve efficiency, I introduced the idea of implementing a design system. This system would enable us to design more efficiently and facilitate rapid experimentation. Before proposing this change, I reviewed the roadmap to ensure that it aligned with our long-term goals. The design team quickly jumped on board by realising its potential.


I also collaborated with the development team to explore how the design system could integrate with our existing projects, and if it could be maintained by both the design and development sides. I began breaking down the product, and mapping out all of the components from simplest to most complex. During this process, we established clear and optimal guidelines for design and implementation.

We reviewed all screens and broke them down into component sections.

An example of the Boom and AmuletoBet navigation structures.

Design

We began designing the components by first looking at the basic elements like typography, colours, inputs, and buttons. These elements were made to detached from any specific context.


We also made sure that our design was inclusive, meaning it considered the needs of people with different types of disabilities, whether they're permanent, temporary, or situational. We checked our current website for any visual or technical issues related to accessibility, following the WCAG 2.1 guidelines, and fixed them in our new design system. This way, Boom will be accessible to as many people as possible in the future.


In terms of the project's scope, we designed it to be flexible so that it can easily include other brands from the Hero portfolio in the future, making our design system adaptable and scalable.


We set up some basic rules for how colours, typography, and spacing should look. Then we reviewed simple atoms like buttons, input fields, and forms. Because we had these simple rules in place, it became easy to create the larger components. Each new component we made therefore followed the same rules and took cues from the components we already had.

The typography styles were defined with generic names instead of specific ones.

Build

On completion I gave a presentation to the design team, and access to the design system was shared. See the Figma file here.

Successes

Design backlog tasks were completed faster, with more time allowed for research and ideation.
