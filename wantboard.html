Case study

Wantboard

Responsibilities

UX design, UI design, prototypes.

Goals

Build trust in the brand.

Deliverable

User interface design.

Building trust in ecommerce

Overview

Despite their proximity to the U.S., Canadians suffer from limitations of choice when online shopping. The Canadian version of Amazon stocks drastically less products than the American version, and many ecommerce sites won’t ship to Canada. This leads to frustration and inconvenience for many Canadians.

Wantboard allows Canadians to buy items that are not available in Canada. This is achieved by storing products in a warehouse in the U.S., close to the Canadian border. Whenever orders are placed, a member of the team drives to the warehouse, bringing the products into Canada by car. Shipping is then done from our office in Canada.

This reduces shipping costs, speeds up delivery times and gives the customer access to more products that are currently only available in the U.S..

The challenge

Create deeper relationships with customers

Our challenge was to evolve with customers and enter the highly competitive ecommerce marketplace. As an early stage startup, we had the following goals:

  • • Build customer confidence in the Wantboard brand.
  • • Offer customers a unique shopping experience.
  • • Build growth through repeat customers and word of mouth.

Competitor analysis

I began by looking at the major ecommerce platforms that the average consumer recognizes easily, including Amazon, Etsy and eBay. Finding nothing noteworthy, I began looking at smaller companies and platforms that the typical consumer may not be as familiar with.

I found an interesting user interface element used by Everlane, an ethical clothing company. Focused on transparency, their website breaks down the cost of a product through an infographic.

Ideas

I didn’t want to bore the customer, and knew the pricing had to be more interesting that a list of numbers. As the fees were built on top on the original product price, I settled on using the idea of a flow diagram, which has potential for more visual stimulation than simply numbers or a table. I began sketching out quick ideas of how to show each cost in a hierarchical fashion. I then narrowed down the idea of a flowchart diagram into an ‘umbrella’ which contained all additional fees. This allowed more freedom for including small illustrations.

Solution

I designed some illustrations and added them to the infographic. In prototyping, I wanted to use the dark red that Wantboard uses in its branding, to attract the reader’s eye. This made the page too heavy, detracting from a main point of focus and losing contrast.

Some design changes helped readability, and I also made changes to some of the illustrations. I added the original price to give a sense of progression.

A third iteration was made. I made slight changes to the illustrations, incorporating the US and Canadian flags into the price tags to reflect cross-border shipping and provide some contrast.

Implementation

This version of the price breakdown is now live on the Wantboard website, giving transparency and context to the customer.
