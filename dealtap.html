Optimizing and streamlining a real estate app

What was the project?

I was hired to understand a product, optimize the user flow and update the user interface.

Company

DealTap

Timeline

Apr 2018 - Apr 2019

Methods

Wireframing, prototype testing, personas, card sorting, competitive analysis, stakeholder interviews, user interviews and testing, UI elements.

Description

Business requirements meant a full redesign of the product was needed. I was hired as product designer to understand the product, optimize the UX flow and update the UI. I worked on this for a year as the only designer.

The challenge

Our users completed their work tasks on different devices in different locations - coffee shops, in their cars, and while talking with clients. This meant ease-of-use of UX and an understandable UI was a priority.

The goal

Improve overall functionality and the user experience compared to our competitors. The product should be easy to pick up and use, taking advantage of existing mental models and design patterns. A product team consisting of a product owner, developers, and myself as designer was formed.

Discover

The product team had a project kickoff where we defined project milestones, aligned on key performance indicators (KPIs) and created a shared product vision. The meeting allowed us to identify return on investment (ROI), find any risks and understand any technical constraints.

The previous design of Dealtap’s homescreen. Business requirements wanted an update.

Notes from the project kickoff meeting.

In terms of UX benchmarking, we wanted to confirm we were offering a good user experience by focusing on a user-centric design process. This required user testing and investigating data analytics.

Work began in agile sprints. I carried out a competitor analysis session, analyzing direct competition in the real estate space, and other products using digital signatures and document sharing features. Screenshots and video recordings of user flows were created to compare design patterns and overall user experience.

My competitor analysis research explored visual hierarchy and design patterns.

With business requirements set up, I needed to find user requirements. I set up sessions over five days for user interviews and usability testing. Six real estate agents were invited, ranging from beginners to advanced ‘power’ users. As facilitator, I managed the sessions with support from the product manager.

Some notes from the first user testing session.

Based on business requirements, I wanted to focus testing on the product’s core user journey. I created a task-completion checklist of:

  • • Creating a new deal document package.
  • • Adding relevant documents to the document package.
  • Adding their digital signature.
  • Sharing documents to a client (dummy email address for testing purposes).

Users were recorded during the testing. We then ran a card-sorting session for feedback on the product’s user journey, and interviewed users with open-ended questions to understand how they used the product.

After the testing was complete, I compared task-completion times and used a mind map to compare interviews. All this data allowed me to identify these pain points:

  • • Beginner users adding digital signatures to documents.
  • • Beginner users not able to keep track of document history.
  • Adding their digital signature.
  • All users unclear around saving documents and digital signatures (and auto-save feature).

These issues were classified as high impact, meaning they prevented users from completing the user journey. Other issues were categorized as moderate or low impact, and added to the product backlog to be fixed at a later date. Overall, the testing indicated areas to focus on:

  • • Creating a new deal document package.
  • • Adding their digital signature to documents.
  • • Sharing documents
  • • Organizing deal document packages
  • • Making the auto-save feature easier to understand

Define

Data from the user interviews allowed me to create three personas. These were used to guide my design decisions when considering user demographics.

I developed three personas based on my user testing interviews.

At this point product management supported me in creating an advisory board of users. Once a month, the board would be invited into a focus group session to give their opinions on product developments.

I began brainstorming an Information Architecture to plan the product structure. We knew our users wanted access to documents quickly, so using the personas was key in deciding how to structure core navigation, and making sure that the functionality was laid out in a way that made sense. Affinity diagramming helped me organize ideas.

A wall after an affinity diagramming exercise I carried out to organize ideas.

Develop

The next step was ideation, using sketching to quickly map out ideas for user flows. Through iteration, I created a flow that influenced the sitemap. This gave me a product overview, and helped me understand the relationships between screens.

Some quick ideation sketches around the document signing flow.

A sketch to help find ideas for the login flow.

I created user story maps based on personas to understand different flows for different users.

Part of a sitemap I created to develop a user flow through interactions and their relations.

A main idea involved reworking the dashboard interface, which would offer quicker access to:

  • • Recent deal packages.
  • • Most used documents.
  • Recent contacts.

I used existing design patterns and UX mental models to explore visual treatments and layouts. My sketches were iterated and developed into digital wireframes to clearly show the user journey.

A wireframe of a document upload feature, using existing mental models and design patterns.

I wanted to test the user journey, and set up another usability testing session over a one‐week period with six users. This helped me decide if functionality met user needs. Feedback was positive, with users appreciating the dashboard interface and emphasis on recent activity.

Happy with the updated UX flow, work began on the UI, and I felt the introduction of a design system would speed up my workflow. I spent a week creating components, prioritizing for mobile devices. I also began work on a styleguide, making decisions on a colour palette and typography.

I created components and began categorizing them into different states and flows.

In terms of UI, legibility was a huge factor, as features had to be easily accessible in the different environments real estate agents work in, including outdoors or low-light conditions.

Deliver

While I was in the stage of working on the UI, business strategies meant that Dealtap was acquired by Remine, a real estate platform based in the U.S..

Result

My UX research was added to Remine’s plans around their product optimization.
