Building an end-to-end enterprise real estate solution

What was the project?

I was hired to understand a product, optimise the user flow and update the UI.

My role

  • — User testing and interviews.
  • — Synthesising data through affinity diagramming.
  • — Information Architecture.
  • — Solution concepts.
  • — Wireframes.
  • — Prototypes.
  • — UI components.

Description

The product team was approached with two objectives — to enhance the app's functionality and usability beyond its competitors, and to design a fresh UI.

The problem

The app needed a makeover in terms of usability and visuals to attract potential investors and users.

The outcome

An improved user journey and updated UI components, and awareness of how our users think and feel about the app.

Research

The product team had a kickoff meeting to define project milestones, align on key performance indicators (KPIs), and create a strategic vision. We also identified potential risks, and gained insights into any technical constraints that could impact the project.


To ensure that our product remained user-centric, I conducted a benchmarking exercise. This involved a competitor analysis session, where I examined products in the real estate domain as well as other products that featured digital signatures and document sharing capabilities. This analysis gave insights into design patterns and the overall user experience, enabling us to refine our approach.

The previous design of Dealtap’s homescreen. Business requirements wanted an update.

In terms of business requirements, our high level goals were to:

  • — Become superior against market leaders.
  • — Build an end-to-end solution for all users.

With a concept of what the new DealTap should be, we had to ask users what they needed. Over the course of a week, we invited in five real estate agents for testing. I set up a series of tasks involving the typical user journey. Participants consisted of real estate agents with different levels of experience with DealTap, from novices to ‘power’ users.


Based on business requirements, I wanted to focus the testing on the product’s core user journey. I created a task-completion checklist of:

  • — Creating a new deal document package.
  • — Adding relevant documents to the document package.
  • — Adding their digital signature.
  • — Sharing documents to a client (dummy email address for testing purposes).

After testing the functionality, we ran a series of card sorting sessions, which allowed us to find users high-level thoughts around the structure of the app. After testing, we interviewed every agent about their process using DealTap and their typical daily work. Users were also asked to imagine their ideal product, with no technical restrictions.

To gain inspiration into features and users needs, Users were asked to imagine their ideal product, thinking of tech in the year 2025.

We then ran a card-sorting session for feedback on the product’s user journey, and interviewed users with open-ended questions to understand how they used the product.


After the testing was complete, I compared task-completion times and used a mind map to categorise the results of the user feedback sessions. All this data allowed me to identify these pain points:

  • — Beginner users adding digital signatures to documents.
  • — Beginner users not able to keep track of document history.
  • — All users unclear around saving documents and digital signatures (and auto-save feature).

These issues were classified as high impact, meaning they prevented users from completing the user journey. Other issues were categorised as moderate or low impact, and added to the product backlog to be fixed at a later date. Overall, the testing indicated areas to focus on:

  • — Creating a new deal document package.
  • — Adding their digital signature to documents.
  • — Organising deal document packages.
  • — Making the auto-save feature easier to understand.

I entered all the ideas into a spreadsheet and prioritised them against our personas needs, tech feasibility, and business objectives. This informed our phasing strategy for the app, and the product feature roadmap.


Data from the user interviews allowed me to create three personas. These were used to guide my design decisions when considering user demographics. At this point product management supported me in creating an advisory board of users. Once a month, the board would be invited into a focus group session to give their opinions on product developments.


I began gathering an Information Architecture to plan the product structure. We knew our users wanted access to documents quickly, so using the personas was key in deciding how to structure core navigation, and making sure that the functionality was laid out in a way that made sense. Affinity diagramming helped me organise and prioritise ideas.

Design

The next step was ideation, using sketching to quickly map out ideas for user flows. Through iteration, I created a sitemap. This gave me a product overview, and helped me understand the relationships between screens.


I used common design patterns and UX mental models to explore visual treatments and layouts. My sketches were iterated and developed into wireframes to clearly show the user journey. I explored different visual treatments and changes in layouts, and how they could affect the typical user journey. Sketching and storyboarding, I generated ideas about the arrangement of UI, functional and data elements, and interactive behaviours.

A sketch to help find ideas for the login flow.

Prototype

I wanted to test the user journey, and set up another usability testing session over a one‐week period with six users. This helped me decide if functionality met user needs. Feedback was positive, with users appreciating the dashboard interface and emphasis on recent activity to reduce cognitive load.

A wireframe of a document upload feature, which was tested with users.

Build

Happy with the updated UX flow, work began on the UI, creating components and prioritising for mobile devices. I also began work on a styleguide, making decisions on a colour palette and typography. While I was in the stage of working on the UI, business strategies meant that Dealtap was acquired by Remine, a real estate platform based in the U.S.. My UX research was added to Remine’s plans around their product optimisation.
