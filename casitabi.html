Simplifying signup to increase conversions

What was the project?

I audited the signup process to find problems, and designed solutions.

My role

  • — User interaction analysis.
  • — Wireframes.
  • — UX audit.
  • — User survey.
  • — UI components.
  • — Quantitative data.

Description

Responding to both business and user needs, I looked into the signup flow of a popular Japanese product, which was suffering becuase of a lack of localised features.

The problem

We noticed in the first week of May that the number of new customers completing the signup flow had been declining for almost 2 weeks, since April 29. The design team was assigned to look into it along with a product manager.

The outcome

Signup conversions increased by 73% post implementation.

Research

Through customer support, we found that our NPS (net promoter score) was 34/100 and in a negative trend. This was a priority to fix for the business. Business goals were set as:

  • — New users: Less friction and more motivation to sign up.
  • — Returning users: Understand that the product is localised to users’ market, to win retention.

Assessing and benchmarking UX

Customer support was getting comments on the signup process as difficult, with users taking 7-10 minutes to sign up.

We carried out a System Usability Scale (SUS) survey through email, with users who had registered their email but not completed the signup flow. The score was a 27, which was classed as an F (failure). 16 people filled out the survey.

The System Usability Scale (SUS). Our signup flow had a score of 27 (F).

This was potentially the reason why people were completing the first step, entering email and password. But not completing the second step.

The signup flow is made of two steps, first entering email and password, and second entering address and contact information

Customer support

We learned from our customer support team that we had been receiving comments about signup being diffcult for one and a half months, since March 22.

UX audit

I reviewed the signup flow, analysed users' movements using Hotjar, and spoke with product management and developers.

As well as looking at Hotjar data, I went through the flow and highlighted findings.

  • — Due to our database setup, users are expected to use English alphabet / characters during the signup process. For a Japan only product, this is not very intuitive and not respectful of our users.
  • — 68% of users spend over one minute entering their birthdate. Because of the way the forms are labelled, these users are not fully clear that they are expected to enter their birthdate.
  • — Users are required to choose their country of residence, even though the only choice available in the dropdown is Japan.
  • — A banner headline is competing against the main signup headline.
  • — Visual spacing/whitespace can be adjusted.

Decision

First priority is reworking database setup to allow for Japanese characters. This would meet our localisation goals.


Second area to improve was address input. Business goals decided to introduce Google Maps API to autofill addresses. Our expectation is Japanese users would then be able to complete the signup flow easier, resulting in less friction.


Third area to improve is adding password validation. This would modernise our product and bring a sense of added security. Potential wins with end-users:

  • — Privacy (email is validated).
  • — Security (password is secure).
  • — Customer service (we take user data seriously).

Fourth area is relabelling date-of-birth form.


Last priority is fixing visual hierarchy issues.

Design

Requirements for the designs were concluded from both the user survey, findings from Hotjar data analytics and ux audit:

  • — Simplify signup process.
  • — Must be mobile-first (90% sessions).
  • — Must be able to clear password and start again after validation.
  • — Password validation must visually show requirements.
  • — Must allow option to add address manually (edge cases).
  • — Clean up visual hierarchy (fonts, whitespace).

Exploration

I used wireframes for quick experimentation with design patterns including layout, hierarchy, and components.

These wireframes show different layouts of the password validation list.

While working on design for password validation, the original idea was to use green text to align with our ‘success’ green. This failed readability testing, so we decided to use black on white for the final UI.

Exploration of colour for the password validation list.

Work progressed from wireframes to the prototypes.

The highest priority area of improvement was making sure Japanese users could use Japanese characters. A backend developer worked on this.

The second priority was incorporating the Google Maps API to autofill addresses based on the postcode. This meant less friction for users.

The third priority was removing the country selector dropdown. This form was redundant and was causing confusion, according to the customer support team.

The fourth priority was adding password validation to ensure a sense of security and trust around the brand.

The fifth priority was simplifying the birthdate form by changing the label.

The sixth priority was adjusting weight of fonts and spacing to improve visual hierarchy.

Validation

Data showed that users understood the list of password requirements, leading to 40% less time spent on this step.

Data showed that address autofill resulted in less calls to customer support, and less frustration when interacting with the form.

Data showed that less time was spent filling the birthdate form, and much less frustration.

Product management was happy with the 73% positive change to signup conversions in the two weeks after implementation.

Before

  • — Not designed for the Japanese market.
  • — SUS score of 27 (F).
  • — NPS score 34/100.

After

  • — Redefined flows, features for localisation.
  • — SUS score of 82 (B).
  • — NPS score 72/100.

Successes

The changes met the user needs for easier signup, and the business goals of more conversions.
