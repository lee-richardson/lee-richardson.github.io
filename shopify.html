Guiding new users with an onboarding feature

What was the project?

How can we help users launching a store? I interviewed users to find a solution.

My role

  • — User interviews and survey.
  • — Competitor analysis.
  • — Prototype testing.
  • — Wireframes.
  • — UI components.
  • — User testing session.

Description

Aligning with business goals of helping new users, I carried out user interviews to find their needs, and designed a new tool focused on onboarding.

The problem

Analytics identified a problem: some stores were being created but then remained inactive, with no products added or sales traffic. It became clear that after some users created accounts, they had problems in finding ideas for what to sell. This was a cause of frustration for users and Shopify.

The outcome

Hi-fidelity prototype of an optional onboarding experience, and new components added to the Polaris design system.

Research

The project began by scoping the task with the team and a business analyst. Together, we set goals, outlined requirements, and calculated the expected return on investment (ROI). My task was to create a solution that effectively communicated Shopify's values of being user-friendly, and aligned with its design principles and content strategy.


To understand the project's technical constraints and expectations, I conducted stakeholder interviews with the team. We identified key performance indicators (KPIs) that included:

  • — Increasing the number of new stores actively adding products.
  • — Increase positive customer feedback.

I had a day-long competitor analysis session, researching e-commerce, trends and SEO tracking. Since the development team expressed interest in scraping data from competitors, my focus was primarily on platforms like AliExpress, Amazon, eBay, and sources such as Google Trends and Google Shopping Insights. The goal was to identify any design patterns and mental models related to user flows.

Some competitor research focusing on e-commerce design patterns.

I held interview sessions with five different users in order to hear how they actually use Shopify, ranging from beginners to seasoned business owners who ran Shopify stores full-time. The success metrics emerged as:

  • — Reduce the risk of users being overwhelmed after setting up a store.
  • — Give users the opportunity to find products that the public demands.
  • — Boost sales for users.

Some rough notes from condensing user interview data into contextual information.

To cater to different user demographics, I created two personas based on interview insights. These personas were given motivations, goals, and frustrations, allowing me to better align my design decisions with their goals.


To understand user needs and potential user journeys, I employed a mind map approach. This allowed me to structure ideas, uncover patterns and highlight key focus areas. Results of this analysis included:

  • — Searching.
  • — Related items.
  • — Categories.
  • — New and trending.
  • — Trust.

Two personas were created to help design decisions.

All this data was then sorted and prioritised. I used affinity diagramming to develop ideas around functionality:

  • — Recommend trending products.
  • — Help users to feel confident that their products will sell.
  • — Suggest product categories and specific products.
  • — Allow users to search for and filter products.
  • — Avoid overwhelming the user with too many options.
  • — Be user-friendly for beginners and mobile users.
  • — Fit into Shopify's main functionality.

Affinity diagramming helped categorise ideas around areas of improvement.

Using SEO, the app would highlight and recommend products that have high search volume, and low competition with other Shopify stores. Basically, products that are in demand and have low presence in online marketplaces.


Iteration of ideas led to the homepage resembling a to-do list, so users can understand where they are in the process and earn a feeling of completion when they complete the steps. This progression would also likely drive users to complete all the steps. Though development of the site map, the user flow emerged:

  • — Browse product categories for one of interest.
  • — Add products from categories to a saved products list.
  • — Customize product information in the saved products list.
  • — Use search volume data to see if chosen products are unique enough to stand out in a marketplace.
  • — Push products to store.
  • — See and register with related available domains and social media accounts.
  • — Start selling.

Early notes showing the user flow idea of a to-do list.

Design

At this point I began paper sketches, allowing for experimentation while considering persona needs. These initial sketches served as a foundation and were refined into wireframes to show interactions.

Sketches around the ideas for interactions and user flows.

Prototype

Once I’d reached a complete user flow, the wireframes were developed into a low-fidelity prototype. To confirm my assumptions, I organised a user testing session involving five participants with varying levels of experience. This diverse group included two individuals who were aware of Shopify but had never used it, representing the demographic of beginner Shopify users.


During the day-long testing session, participants were presented with a task-completion list, and their interactions with the prototype were observed and analysed.

A wireframe prototype with basic functionality of the user flow.

After testing, it became clear that some parts of the user flow needed to be simplified. The idea of a product shortlist seemed to be unnecessary, and I decided it should be removed for the initial MVP and reconsidered after more research. After the flow of the design had been updated I then used InVision to build a high-fidelity prototype.


To validate the changes and gather more feedback, another user feedback session was conducted. Five participants were presented with a task-completion list that reflected the user journey. The feedback received included:

  • — Positive feedback from new users who appreciated the tool for providing product ideas to sell.
  • — Users acknowledging that the feature was introduced at an appropriate point in the user flow.
  • — Recognition that the focus on trending search volumes enabled users to tap into popular product trends effectively.

Build

UI work began, and I collaborated with the development team to confirm the user flow and UI. Any new components I created were added into Shopify’s existing Polaris design system.


As the design work progressed, a key team member left Shopify. A decision was made to discontinue the project based on evolving business requirements. As a result, my ongoing work was absorbed into the strategic backlog for potential future product improvements.

A view of the work-in-progress UI.

Successes

We gained knowledge into the thoughts and feelings of new Shopify users, and found a solution that eases potential cognitive overload.
