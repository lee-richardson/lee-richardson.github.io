Designed an onboarding tool

2017 - 2018

Shopify

Methods ↴

Stakeholder interviews, user interviews, competitive analysis, card-sorting, wireframing, prototype testing, UI design.

Description ↴

Aligning with business needs, I carried out research to find user needs, and designed a new tool aimed at beginner users. Working in agile sprints, the project took almost a year, and I worked as the only designer on a product team.

The challenge ↴

Through analytics, Shopify knew some stores were being created but remaining inactive, without any products being added or sales traffic. It seemed that after creating an account, some users had trouble finding ideas of what to sell. Clearly, this was a cause of frustration for Shopify and its users.

The goal ↴

Utilize user research to find issues and create a tool that offers ideas of what to sell. As part of the larger Shopify product, the solution should share the same vision and design principles of Shopify.

Define

Notes from the kickoff meeting setting out business requirements.

The team kicked off the project with a business analyst to set up goals, requirements and return on investment (ROI). I had to find a solution that shared the vision, design principles and content strategy of Shopify.

I carried out stakeholder interviews with the product team, consisting of two project managers, two SEO specialists and three back-end developers. These interviews were to find technical constraints and project expectations. Key Performance Indicators (KPI) included:

  • • Increase stores that are actively adding products.
  • • Increase positive customer feedback.

Notes from the stakeholder interviews around product vision and goals.

I began a day-long competitor analysis session. This was to find any common design patterns and mental models in terms of user flows.

Some competitor research focusing on e-commerce design patterns.

An analytics tool provided the data needed for quantitative research, but I needed to find user insights through qualitative research. To explore user requirements, I facilitated interviews over two days with five users, from beginners to business owners running a Shopify store full-time.

Some rough notes from condensing user interview data into contextual information.

To help target different user demographics, I created two personas based on patterns found in my interview insights. These were given motivations, goals and frustrations. I worked to empathetically align with both personas in order to meet the user goals.

Some rough notes from condensing user interview data into contextual information.

I used a mind map to identify user flow issues and an overview of user needs. This was useful to see if any patterns emerged and to find inspiration of what to focus on. Results included:

  • • Searching.
  • • Related items.
  • • Categories.
  • • New and trending.
  • • Trust.

Develop

With quantitative and qualitative data gathered, I used affinity diagramming to develop ideas around functionality:

  • • Recommend trending products.
  • • Help users to feel confident that their products will sell.
  • • Suggest product categories and specific products.
  • • Allow users to search for and filter products.
  • • Avoid overwhelming the user with too many options.
  • • Be user-friendly for beginners and mobile users.
  • • Fit into Shopify's main functionality.

Affinity diagramming helped categorize ideas for functionality.

Using SEO, the app would highlight and recommend products that have high search volume, and low competition with other Shopify stores. Basically, products that are in demand and have low presence in online marketplaces.

Iteration of ideas led to the homepage resembling a to-do list, so users can understand where they are in the process and earn a feeling of completion when they complete the steps. This progression would also likely drive users to complete all the steps.

Early notes showing the user flow idea of a to-do list.

Though development of the site map, the user flow emerged:

  • • Browse product categories for one of interest.
  • • Add products from categories to a saved products list.
  • • Customize product information in the saved products list.
  • • Use search volume data to see if chosen products are unique enough to stand out in a marketplace.
  • • Push products to store.
  • • See and register with related available domains and social media accounts.
  • • Start selling.

Design

Sketches around the ideas for interactions and user flows.

With functionality laid out, I needed ideas around the user flow. At this point I began sketching, allowing for experimentation while considering persona needs. Once I’d reached a complete user flow, the sketches were cleaned up and developed into a low-fidelity prototype. I facilitated a user testing session with five people at different levels of experience. Two people were aware of Shopify but not users, to capture the demographic of beginner Shopify users. A task-completion list was provided, and the testing session lasted a day.

A wireframe prototype with basic functionality of the user flow.

An expanded view of the wireframe prototypes.

After testing, it became clear that some parts of the user flow needed to be simplified. The idea of a shortlist seemed to be unnecessary, and I decided it should be removed for the initial MVP and reconsidered after more research. After the flow of the design had been updated I refined the prototype, and presented results with the team.

The product had overall positive feedback in the final testing session. This involved a task-completion list involving adding finding a category idea, adding products registering a domain. Feedback included:

  • • New users appreciating the tool offering ideas for products to sell.
  • • Users understanding that the tool came at a correct time in the user onboarding flow.
  • • The focus on trending search volumes allowed users to utilize popular products.

Screenshots showing the development of the UI, and how the design aligns with the larger Shopify product.

UI work began, and I collaborated with the development team to provide components and review development work to confirm the user flow and UI. Any new components I created were added into Shopify’s existing design system.

Deliver

Design was continuing and development sprints began until a key member of the team left. Business requirements decided work should discontinue, and my research was absorbed into Shopify’s UX team’s work.
